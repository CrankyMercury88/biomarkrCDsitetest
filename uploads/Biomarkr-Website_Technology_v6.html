System Overview: Five Integrated Elements

Q-SENS combines optical biosensing, microfluidics, disposable cartridges, cloud computing, and mobile communications into a single diagnostic ecosystem. A palm-sized reader interrogates a single-use cartridge, then streams raw optical data to the cloud, where it is converted into calibrated, longitudinal results and delivered to the patient and clinician through a mobile application.

Q-SENS test flow — from fingerstick to clinical insight
Each fingerstick cartridge is read by the palm-sized reader, which streams raw optical signal to the cloud. Calibration, quality control, and the AI models all live in software, so the platform keeps improving without any hardware change. Device illustrations are schematic renderings.
01
Disposable Test Cartridge

Single-use cartridge integrating the optical sensing chip, reagent reservoirs, sample-processing components, fluidic network, calibration reagents, and a waste chamber. Single use eliminates cross-contamination and simplifies workflow.

02
Optical Sensing Chip

A silicon-based planar waveguide with a functionalized sensing surface and multiple independent sensing regions, read by fluorescence detection. Fabricated with semiconductor-compatible processes for low-cost mass production.

03
Portable Reader

Battery-powered, palm-sized reader housing the excitation light source, optical coupling, photodetectors, fluidic-control electronics, and the cartridge interface. It interrogates the chip and transmits data wirelessly to the cloud.

04
Cloud Infrastructure

Performs signal processing, calibration, quality control, secure data storage, longitudinal patient tracking, and clinical reporting. Over-the-air updates let the platform evolve without touching the hardware.

05
Mobile Application

Handles test initiation, user authentication, result viewing, data management, physician communication, and population analytics, turning each measurement into a tracked point on a personal trajectory.

03 —

The Core Technology: Evanescent-Wave Biosensing

Q-SENS uses a planar optical waveguide that confines light within a thin layer on the chip. While most of the light stays inside the waveguide, a small portion extends just beyond the surface as an evanescent field that penetrates only about 50 to 150 nm. This short reach is the central trick of the platform: it selectively excites fluorescent labels bound at the surface while leaving everything deeper in the sample dark.

The evanescent field reaches only about 50 to 150 nanometers. An antibody is 10 to 15 nm. A red blood cell is 6,000 to 8,000 nm. Surface-bound signal is excited; blood cells sit outside the light entirely. That is what makes direct whole-blood testing possible.

Selective surface excitation — why whole blood works without separation
Only labels within roughly 50 to 150 nanometers of the surface are excited, so background fluorescence from cells and bulk plasma is dramatically reduced. The result is laboratory-grade sensitivity from an unprocessed whole-blood drop.

Beyond this optical selectivity, additional proprietary cartridge design further directs blood cells away from the sensing surface, sharpening the signal-to-background advantage. Patent pending.

Each sensing region carries an immobilized capture antibody specific to one analyte, and the assay is a standard fluorescence sandwich. The five-step sequence below runs entirely inside the cartridge.

1
Capture antibody immobilization

Analyte-specific antibodies are pre-bound to defined sensing regions on the chip (for example IL-6, TNF-α, IFN-γ, IL-10, IL-1β), each region measuring one biomarker.

2
Sample introduction

Whole blood enters the cartridge and target biomarkers bind to their capture antibodies. No centrifugation or serum separation is required.

3
Detection antibody binding

Fluorescently labeled detection antibodies bind the captured analytes, forming the sandwich: capture antibody → biomarker → detection antibody.

4
Optical excitation

The waveguide excites only the fluorophores near the surface, so signal comes overwhelmingly from genuine surface-bound complexes rather than free label in the bulk sample.

5
Quantitation

Measured fluorescence intensity is converted to analyte concentration through calibration algorithms applied in the cloud.

Multiplex Architecture: 32 Measurements on One Chip

The waveguide matrix crosses 4 optical input channels with 8 output channels, creating 4 × 8 = 32 independent sensing locations on a single chip. That capacity is spent on analytes, replicates, controls, and on-chip calibration standards, so a single fingerstick can return a full multiplexed panel with built-in quality control.

05 —

Platform Attributes

Each design choice removes a barrier that keeps cytokine testing locked inside centralized laboratories.

Direct whole blood
No centrifugation, no serum separation

Conventional cytokine assays need serum or plasma and a centrifuge. Q-SENS reads a 10 µL fingerstick drop directly, which also preserves cytokines that degrade rapidly after collection, improving biological accuracy.

Sensitivity
Single-digit pg/mL detection

The platform targets single-digit pg/mL sensitivity for cytokines such as IL-6, TNF-α, and IFN-γ, approaching or exceeding many centralized laboratory systems.

Self-calibration
Standards built into every cartridge

Each sensing site is measured in two steps: first the unknown sample, then an on-cartridge standard. This per-sensor calibration, together with internal controls and reference channels carried inside every cartridge, reduces reader-to-reader and cartridge-to-cartridge variation, improves reproducibility, and lowers maintenance for the user.

Speed
Results in under 10 minutes

Typical turnaround is roughly 5 to 10 minutes at the point of need, fast enough for in-visit decisions and for serial monitoring across hours where cytokines move quickly.

Manufacturing
Semiconductor-compatible fabrication

The silicon photonic chip is built with semiconductor-compatible processes, supporting low-cost, high-volume manufacturing as the menu and install base scale.

Setting
Decentralized and at home

The same device fits emergency departments, clinics, physician offices, resource-limited settings, and the home, which is the platform's primary frame for chronic inflammation monitoring.

06 —

Cloud and AI Infrastructure

The reader is deliberately thin. It captures raw optical signal and hands the heavy work to the cloud, where every measurement is processed, calibrated, quality-checked, and stored against the patient's history. This is what turns Q-SENS from a testing device into a monitoring platform: the value is the trajectory, not the single reading.

Stage 1
Signal extraction
Raw optical signal is cleaned of optical artifacts and corrected for nonspecific background fluorescence.
Stage 2
Calibration + QC
Calibration curves convert intensity to concentration; automated quality control validates each assay using on-chip standards.
Stage 3
Longitudinal data layer
Each result is stored against the patient's history, building a personal, circadian-aware cytokine baseline over time.
Stage 4
AI trajectory models
Models read rate of change and deviation from personal baseline to flag immune events and surface clinical reports.

Because the intelligence lives in software, analytical performance improves continuously without recalling or modifying a single device. Calibration refinements, new quality-control rules, and better predictive models reach the entire install base through over-the-air updates.

Personal baselines
Deviation, not absolute value

Repeated measurement establishes each individual's cytokine baseline, so future readings are interpreted as movement relative to that person rather than against a population reference range.

Trajectory signal
Rate of change and dynamics

Rate of change, time-to-peak, resolution velocity, and cytokine ratios encode information invisible to any single measurement. The cloud computes these features across the longitudinal record.

Predictive models
Pre-symptomatic detection

Models trained on personal trajectories aim to detect immune deviations before clinical symptoms appear, the same logic that made continuous glucose monitoring more useful than isolated glucose readings.

App + reporting
Patient and clinician views

The mobile application delivers results, trends, and alerts to patients while routing clinical reports and population analytics to physicians, closing the loop from fingerstick to decision.

Snapshot vs. trajectory — why frequent measurement changes the signal
Illustrative. The same absolute value carries opposite meaning depending on direction and rate of change. A lab snapshot sees one dot; cloud-tracked longitudinal monitoring sees the curve and the deviation from an individual baseline.
